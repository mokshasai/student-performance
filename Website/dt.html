Overview

A Decision Tree is a supervised machine learning model that learns a series of if-then-else rules from training data to classify or predict an outcome. Starting from a root node, the algorithm recursively splits the data on the feature that best separates the classes — typically using the Gini impurity or information gain (entropy) criterion. Each internal node represents a decision based on a single feature value, and each leaf node represents a predicted class label. Decision trees are highly interpretable: the rules they learn can be visualized as a flowchart that anyone can follow to understand how a prediction was made. This transparency makes them especially valuable in educational contexts, where the reasoning behind a classification ("this student is predicted to score Low because they have 3 prior failures and study less than 2 hours per week") is just as important as the prediction itself. However, unconstrained decision trees can overfit to training data, so parameters like max_depth, min_samples_split, and min_samples_leaf are used to regularize the model.

In the context of student performance, a Decision Tree is applied to classify each student into one of three performance categories — Low (G3 < 10), Medium (10 ≤ G3 < 14), and High (G3 ≥ 14) — based on their behavioral and demographic attributes. The model can reveal which features the tree splits on most often, providing an interpretable ranking of feature importance. For example, if the root split is always on "number of past failures," it strongly suggests that prior academic history is the most discriminative factor for predicting final grade outcomes. Decision Trees also handle mixed data types (numeric and categorical) well, and require minimal preprocessing compared to distance-based models like KMeans or SVM.

🌳

Why Decision Trees? They produce human-readable rules, handle both numeric and categorical features, require no normalization, and directly show which features matter most — making them ideal for explaining academic predictions to non-technical stakeholders such as teachers and school administrators.
